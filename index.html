Who Powers the Energy Transition?

Electric vehicles, batteries, and clean technologies rely on cobalt and other critical minerals. Around the world, the people who extract these resources often face severe challenges, including child labor, forced labor, armed conflict near mines, extreme poverty, and exposure to toxic substances. MineSense highlights these human and environmental impacts while placing them in the broader context of global mineral extraction.

DRC & cobalt: No country is more central to the battery economy’s human cost. Tens of thousands of children work in or near mines; industrial and artisanal cobalt production sit alongside conflict-affected regions; communities report contaminated water, reproductive health crises, and daily wages that can fall below two dollars. This is not a side issue for the energy transition—it is one of its defining injustices.

Critical Minerals at a Glance

Cobalt (Democratic Republic of the Congo)

Cobalt powers EV batteries and electronics; the Democratic Republic of the Congo produces roughly 70% of the world’s supply. Artisanal and industrial mining overlap with extreme poverty, documented child labor, forced labor concerns, and armed-group presence in eastern regions. Communities face contaminated water, health crises, and wages that can be under two dollars a day. Read the full country report for more detail.

Lithium

Drives the battery revolution. Chile’s Atacama salt flats and Argentina’s brine pools are iconic sources, but extraction can draw down scarce water in deserts where Indigenous communities rely on the same aquifers. Australia’s hard-rock mines and refining in China and elsewhere add new layers of energy use and land use. Bolivia holds untapped reserves. Across these regions, disputes over land rights, water access, and who benefits from the transition remain unresolved.

Rare Earth Elements

Critical for magnets in EVs and wind turbines, electronics, and defense systems. China dominates mining and especially refining, which concentrates geopolitical risk and environmental burden in specific industrial zones. Smaller producers (e.g. Myanmar, Australia) raise separate questions about governance, labor, and habitat damage. The full chain from mine to magnet often lacks transparency for consumers and policymakers alike.

Coltan

Used in capacitors for phones and laptops. The Democratic Republic of the Congo and Rwanda are key sources. Eastern DRC has long been associated with conflict minerals: armed groups, informal taxation of trade routes, and violence near mining areas. Due diligence and traceability schemes exist, but implementation remains uneven and communities still report insecurity and exploitation.
